Powering the Future for All

An Exploration of UN Sustainable Development Goal #7

By Alex Doe

My Interest: The Spark of Innovation and Progress

The UN Sustainable Development Goal that I am most interested in is **Goal #7**, ensuring access to affordable, reliable, sustainable, and modern energy for all. To me, energy means work. It represents our ability to accomplish tasks without arduous manual labor, to power new technologies, and to spread education to every corner of the globe. This isn't just an abstract concept for me; I've contributed in the past by inventing a battery that runs on organic waste. The simple act of filling it with any sort of organic material allows it to produce electricity. Through this research, I learned that we have made incredible headway on this goal over the past few years. It's become clear that we are very likely to get **immeasurably close to solving it**, and that progress is what truly motivates me.

This **Donut Chart** shows the remaining population gap in 2021. The immense progress we've made only makes the concentration of the remaining challenge (particularly in Sub-Saharan Africa) more striking. This visualization highlights exactly where our Target 7.b efforts must be concentrated.

The **Line Chart** clearly maps the rapid acceleration of energy access globally since 2010. This trend reinforces my belief that achieving the goal is possible; the rate of progress over the last decade shows a powerful trajectory we must maintain, especially in the least developed regions.

A Familiar Context: The Appalachian Energy Divide

To understand Target 7.b—"expand infrastructure and upgrade technology for supplying modern and sustainable energy services"—I look to a familiar context: the **Appalachian regions** of Ohio and neighboring states. While not a "developing country," many rural communities here face a form of energy poverty. They are often served by an aging electrical grid that is less reliable and more susceptible to outages, and their local economies are historically tied to high-carbon sources. The people in these towns are directly affected: unreliable power impacts small businesses, online education, and access to telehealth services. Addressing this is a pathway to economic revitalization and energy resilience.

This **Grouped Bar Chart** compares the estimated Appalachian region's energy generation mix against the national average. The data clearly illustrates the disproportionately high reliance on coal in this familiar context, underscoring why local infrastructure investment must focus on diversification and adopting modern, sustainable technologies.

The **Radar Chart** visualizes the multidimensional impact of energy insecurity in Appalachia. The low scores in 'Grid Reliability' and 'Affordable Access' show the technical gap, while the lower 'Economic Diversity' score highlights the deep **sociotechnical complexity** of the challenge. Our solution must address all these facets, not just the technical ones.

The Plan: Community Bio-Energy Hubs

My plan is to build on my previous work and address the needs of the Appalachian context directly. I propose the development of small-scale, community-owned **"Bio-Energy Hubs."** These hubs would utilize technology similar to my organic waste battery, converting local organic waste (agricultural runoff, forestry byproducts) into a reliable source of electricity for the community. This creates a closed-loop system that reduces landfill waste, generates clean energy, and provides local jobs.

The target of "expanding infrastructure and upgrading technology" is something I find deeply inspiring. Regions like Appalachia have a unique chance to build a renewable-based infrastructure from the ground up. By making strategic investments now, we can help them leapfrog the fossil fuel-dependent stage entirely. For me, 2030 isn't a final deadline; it's a **continuing promise and effort**, and this plan is a tangible step I can take right now.

Implementation Flow: A Sociotechnical Roadmap

1. Sociological Audit & Design

Community Organizers and Sociologists establish trust and cooperative ownership.

↓

2. Technical & Waste Assessment

Engineers and Planners assess local waste streams and optimal site location.

↓

3. Financial Modeling & Policy Integration

Economists and Policy Experts secure funding and navigate regulatory hurdles.

↓

4. Deployment & Local Training

Engineers install the hub; local workforce is trained for maintenance and operation.
